When art and causes collide

Nobody loves a Renaissance man more than I. It's better yet, when they have a heart, a giving nature and talent that defies at least my explanation. THAT, Ladies and Gentlemen, is unique.

I give you Don Patron. Many of you know him or of him. His genres include everything from fashion to photography; event design to fine art. While most of us were playing in sandboxes and finger painting at the age of seven, Don was selling his first work of art. As an adult, his core career is based on turning social issues into art; art used as a spiritual and charitable platform. He has donated works to raise funds for everything from Knock Out Abuse to Washington Humane/Fashion for Paws. His craft also includes commissions by individuals as well as collections and portraits for government officials.

The creative process begins with the annual State of the Union address where the artist creates a list of issues covered.

He then takes these, extrapolates the causes that resonate with him. These become the basis for his collections which have included wounded soldiers from Iraq and Afghanistan, children living and dying with cancer and the lives of some of Washington DC’s homeless community. His work has been exhibited world-wide.

In a recent display which he entitled the Stellar Constellation Collection hosted by Art Soiree Co. and hosted by its founders Sandro and Tati that premiered this week at L2 in Georgetown, Patron presented a collection of contemporary portraits in watercolor and pastel of 40 local philanthropists that have touched his life and connected him with some of the causes he has supported or will support in the coming year. He keeps names of these people filed both in his memory and on paper, and then selects from his vast list for his subjects. He alternates these shows yearly between DC and New York. The portraits will be on display at L2 in Cady’s Alley for the next six weeks.

What sets Don apart is not only his diversity in style and in genre, but also in his mission has been to bring more truths about the world that we live in to the community in a beautiful way. Patron applies his gift of hearing color and seeing sound, as a way to associate feelings and emotions with colors and, contributing to his passion for capturing humanism and bring vibrant movement and light to all of his pieces. His work is generally never signed as he believes it has moved “beyond ego;” although Patron did, in fact, sign the Constellation works stating "it was my honor to do so." He explains that his art “serves as an American voice who owns the truth; the image as an honest point of view.” In a sense, it’s all very Washingtonian; like he is running for office on some new platform with every collection; except unlike the typical politician, his voice is always startlingly unfeigned.

It can be said that art is in the eye of the beholder—it is subjective. But few can argue that when it speaks to causes that are so stunningly non self-serving; when it celebrates both the light and darkness of emotion; of social issues; of humanity at its best and worst; when it has tangibly contributed to society, then perhaps it has served its highest purpose.
